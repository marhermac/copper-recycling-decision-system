# Key Discoveries

## Project Evolution

This project started with a simple observation:

> Every weekend, trucks drive through urban neighborhoods in Argentina buying old appliances, motors, cables, batteries, and scrap metal.

The initial business question was:

> How profitable is the copper recycling business?

Through data analysis, field observations, and business reasoning, several key discoveries emerged.

---

# Discovery 1

## The business is driven by observation, not theory

The project originated from real-world observations of urban recycling activities rather than from publicly available datasets or academic exercises.

This allowed the project to develop business hypotheses based on actual market behavior.

---

# Discovery 2

## Copper prices experienced significant growth

Analysis of historical copper prices in Argentina suggests a strong upward trend during the last two years, increasing the attractiveness of copper recovery activities.

---

# Discovery 3

## Urban recyclers create value through labor

Urban recyclers invest time and manual work to recover copper from discarded appliances and electrical components.

Their competitive advantage is labor-intensive material recovery.

---

# Discovery 4

## Scrap dealers create value through capital and aggregation

Field observations suggest that neighborhood scrap dealers:

* Purchase recovered materials immediately.
* Operate with limited working capital.
* Accumulate material volume.
* Sell periodically to larger operators.

Their business model appears to rely on liquidity and scale.

---

# Discovery 5

## The copper supply chain is more complex than expected

The observed supply chain appears to be:

Urban Recycler
↓
Neighborhood Scrap Dealer
↓
Wholesaler / Aggregator
↓
Industrial Processor
↓
Foundry / Export Market

Each participant captures value through different mechanisms.

---

# Discovery 6

## Inventory accumulation may increase profitability

Scenario analysis suggests that delaying sales during favorable market conditions can increase profitability.

The decision depends on:

* Expected market prices.
* Storage capacity.
* Liquidity requirements.
* Risk tolerance.

---

# Discovery 7

## The recycler does not search for copper directly

Field observations revealed that recyclers rarely search for copper itself.

Instead, they search for objects that contain recoverable copper.

Examples include:

* Washing machines.
* Dryers.
* Microwave transformers.
* Refrigerators.
* Air conditioners.

---

# Discovery 8

## Electric motors are the true economic unit

The most important finding of the project is that urban recyclers appear to optimize for the acquisition of electric motors rather than for copper alone.

Electric motors provide:

* Predictable copper content.
* High recovery value.
* Easy identification.
* Broad availability.

---

# Discovery 9

## Urban recycling resembles urban mining

The project evolved from a recycling analysis into an urban mining analysis.

The economic process can be described as:

Urban Waste
↓
Asset Identification
↓
Motor Recovery
↓
Copper Extraction
↓
Inventory Accumulation
↓
Sale

---

# Discovery 10

## Air conditioners may represent the highest-value urban mining asset

Based on field expertise, air conditioning units appear to contain the highest amount of recoverable copper among common household appliances.

This suggests that not all urban waste assets have equal economic value.

---

# Final Observation

This project evolved from a simple question:

> "Is copper recycling a good business?"

into a broader research question:

> "Can data analytics help understand and optimize urban mining economics in Argentina?"
